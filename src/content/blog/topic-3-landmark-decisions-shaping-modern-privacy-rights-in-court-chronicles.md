# Topic 3: Landmark Decisions Shaping Modern Privacy Rights

In the ever-evolving landscape of technology and law, privacy rights stand as a cornerstone of democratic society. This third topic in our Court Chronicles series examines key judicial decisions that have profoundly impacted how courts interpret and protect personal privacy in the digital era.

## The Katz v. United States Revolution (1967)

One cannot discuss privacy without starting with *Katz v. United States*. This Supreme Court case expanded the Fourth Amendment's protections beyond physical intrusions to include electronic eavesdropping. Charles Katz's conviction for illegal gambling was overturned because FBI agents had recorded his phone calls without a warrant. Justice Potter Stewart's famous concurrence declared, "The Fourth Amendment protects people, not places." This ruling established the 'reasonable expectation of privacy' test, a framework still used today to navigate surveillance in our connected world.

## From Wiretapping to Digital Surveillance: The Carpenter v. United States Case (2018)

Fast-forward to the smartphone era, and *Carpenter v. United States* marked a significant victory for privacy advocates. The Court ruled 5-4 that the government generally needs a warrant to access historical cell phone location records. Timothy Carpenter's data was used to convict him of robbery, but the justices recognized that such records reveal intimate details of our lives—movements, habits, and associations. Chief Justice John Roberts wrote, "We decline to grant the state unrestricted access to a wireless carrier’s database of physical location information."

This decision has ripple effects, challenging law enforcement's reliance on third-party data and prompting debates on balancing security with civil liberties.

## Emerging Challenges: Data Privacy in the Age of Big Tech

Recent cases like those involving social media platforms highlight ongoing tensions. For instance, rulings on Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act have shielded companies from liability, but calls for reform grow louder amid data breaches and algorithmic biases. Courts are increasingly grappling with questions of consent, surveillance capitalism, and the right to be forgotten.
